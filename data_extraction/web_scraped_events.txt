Below is a chronological list of major, well-documented events in Troy, NY, each with at least one direct online source you can check. I’ve focused on events I could clearly corroborate; this isn’t literally every event in Troy’s history, but it’s meant to be broad and source-grounded rather than speculative.

Before 1800
Pre-1600s – Mohican homeland on the Hudson
The area that is now Troy was part of the homelands of the Mohican (Mahican) people along the upper Hudson River.
Source: Troy, New York – Kids Kiddle (Kiddle)
1609 – Henry Hudson sails past the future Troy
Henry Hudson explores the Hudson River for the Dutch in 1609, passing the site of modern Troy and opening the region to Dutch colonial claims.
Source: History of Troy, NY – LocalWiki (LocalWiki)
1630s – Land becomes part of the Manor of Rensselaerswyck
The region east of the Hudson, including the future Troy, is granted as the patroonship of Rensselaerswyckto Kiliaen van Rensselaer.
Sources:
Rensselaerswyck – Wikipedia (Wikipedia)
History of Troy, NY – LocalWiki (LocalWiki)
c. 1707 – Van der Heyden family farm at “Ferry Hook”
Dirck (Derick) Van der Heyden acquires land and establishes a farm and ferry on the east bank of the Hudson, an early European settlement nucleus for what becomes Troy.
Source: “History of Troy, NY” summarizing early Van der Heyden settlement: LocalWiki – History of Troy, NY (LocalWiki)
January 5, 1789 – The community adopts the name “Troy”
Residents of the small settlement then known as Vanderheyden’s Ferry / Ashley’s Ferry vote to rename the place “Troy,” after the ancient city.
Sources:
Troy, New York – Wikipedia (Wikipedia)
19th-century reference reprinting the 1789 notice: The American Cyclopædia entry for Troy(Wikisource)
1791 – Town of Troy created
The Town of Troy is formed from part of the Manor of Rensselaerswyck; it includes what later becomes Brunswick and Grafton.
Source: Troy, New York – Wikipedia (Wikipedia)
1801 – Incorporated as a village
Troy is chartered as a village.
Source: Troy, New York – Wikipedia (Wikipedia)
1816 – Chartered as a city
Troy receives a city charter and becomes the City of Troy.
Source: Troy, New York – Wikipedia (Wikipedia)

1800–1849: Education, industry & “’Twas the Night Before Christmas”
1820 – “Detachable collar” established in Troy
A Mrs. Montague in Troy (commonly identified as Hannah Lord Montague) cuts the collars off her husband’s shirts so she can launder just the collars and reattach them, an innovation credited with starting the local detachable collar industry that earns Troy its “Collar City” nickname.
Sources:
Troy, New York – Economy section (detachable collar “first established in Troy in 1820 by a Mrs. Montague”) (Wikipedia)
Exploring Troy – Hudson River Valley Institute (HRVI)
1820 – “Great Fire of 1820”
A major fire in Troy destroys large sections of the business district; contemporary and later accounts refer to it as the “great fire of 1820.”
Sources:
Biographical and local histories referencing the “great fire of 1820”: Hudson-Mohawk Genealogical Memoirs – Warren (schenectadyhistory.org)
19th-century Troy history noting the fire and rebuilding: Troy and Rensselaer County, New York; a history (scan) (Internet Archive)
1821 – Troy Female Seminary founded
Emma Hart Willard moves her pioneering girls’ school to Troy and establishes the Troy Female Seminary, the first U.S. institution to offer women an education comparable to that of college-educated men.
Sources:
Emma Willard School – official history (emmawillard.org)
Troy Female Seminary – Britannica (Encyclopedia Britannica)
1823 – “A Visit from St. Nicholas” first published in Troy
The poem better known as “’Twas the Night Before Christmas” is first printed anonymously as “Account of a Visit from St. Nicholas” in the Troy Sentinel on December 23, 1823.
Sources:
Facsimile & transcription of the original Sentinel printing: Merrycoz – 1823 Troy Sentinel text(merrycoz.org)
History.com – “A Visit From St. Nicholas” first published (HISTORY)
1824 – Rensselaer School (now RPI) founded
Stephen Van Rensselaer III establishes the Rensselaer School in Troy, which evolves into Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute (RPI), one of the earliest engineering schools in the U.S.
Sources:
RPI institutional history via Emma Willard article and related references: Emma Willard School – History section mentioning RPI (Wikipedia)
Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute – Wikipedia (Internet Archive)
1830s–1840s – Burden Iron Works grows as major iron producer
The Burden Iron Works complex in South Troy becomes a major iron manufacturing center, later famed for the gigantic Burden Water Wheel and for producing most of the Union Army’s horseshoes in the Civil War.
Sources:
Burden Iron Works – Wikipedia (Wikipedia)
Burden Iron Works – Albany Institute / Hudson Mohawk Gateway (albanyinstitute.org)

1850–1899: “Collar City,” early unions & big league baseball
1862 – Great Fire of Troy
A catastrophic fire on May 10, 1862 destroys many blocks of Troy’s downtown and riverfront warehouses; it is often cited as the “Great Fire of Troy.”
Source: Historical overviews of Troy mentioning the 1862 fire: History of Troy, NY – LocalWiki(LocalWiki)
1864 – Collar Laundry Union formed in Troy
Shirt-collar laundress Kate Mullany organizes the Collar Laundry Union in Troy, widely recognized as the first all-female labor union in the United States.
Sources:
Collar Laundry Union – Wikipedia (Wikipedia)
Kate Mullany House – official site (katemullanynhs.org)
Late 1800s – Troy becomes known as the “Collar City”
Driven by firms such as Cluett, Peabody & Co., Troy manufactures the vast majority of America’s detachable collars and shirt fronts, earning the nickname “Collar City.”
Sources:
Exploring Troy – Hudson River Valley Institute (HRVI)
“Troy: The Collar City” – High Collar Magazine (HIGH COLLAR MAGAZINE)
Kate Mullany article – Hudson Valley Magazine (Hudson Valley Magazine)
1871 – Troy Haymakers play in the first pro baseball league
The Troy Haymakers become a charter member of the National Association of Professional Base Ball Players, the first professional baseball league.
Sources:
1871 Troy Haymakers season – Wikipedia (Wikipedia)
Historical essay: “The Troy Trojans: A Forgotten Part of New York’s Pastime” – Hudson River Valley Institute PDF (HRVI)
1879–1882 – Troy Trojans in the National League
The Troy Trojans play four seasons in Major League Baseball’s National League (1879–1882), giving Troy a brief stint as a major-league city.
Sources:
Troy Trojans (National League) – Wikipedia (Wikipedia)
Historical overview: Troy Trojans – RetroSeasons (Retro Seasons)

1900–1945: Expansion, water supply & higher education
January 1, 1901 – Lansingburgh annexed to the City of Troy
The Village of Lansingburgh is annexed to Troy under a bill signed by Governor Theodore Roosevelt on April 25, 1900; the law takes effect January 1, 1901, greatly expanding the city’s territory and population.
Source: Lansingburgh Historical Society – Annexation to City of Troy(lansingburghhistoricalsocietyarchives.org)
1900–1906 – Tomhannock Reservoir built; 1906 – goes into service
Construction of the Tomhannock Reservoir, northeast of Troy, begins around 1900; it enters service in 1906as the city’s main water supply.
Source: Tomhannock Reservoir – Wikipedia (Wikipedia)
1916 – Russell Sage College founded in Troy
Philanthropist Olivia Slocum Sage founds Russell Sage College in Troy in 1916 as a women’s college, part of a broader legacy of education in the city.
Sources:
Russell Sage – Wikipedia (Legacy section) (Wikipedia)
College catalog noting founding year: Russell Sage College Catalog 2020–21 (PDF) (catalog.sage.edu)
1933 – “Sanforization” process invented by Sanford Cluett of Troy
Sanford Cluett of Troy invents Sanforization, a process for pre-shrinking cotton fabric that becomes an important innovation in the textile industry.
Source summarizing Cluett and Sanforization: History of Troy, NY – LocalWiki (LocalWiki)

1946–1999: Industry shifts, Uncle Sam & sports
1954 – RPI wins its first NCAA men’s ice hockey championship
The 1953–54 RPI Bachelors (Rensselaer Polytechnic Institute) win the 1954 NCAA Men’s Ice Hockey Tournament, bringing Troy its first national college hockey title.
Sources:
1953–54 RPI Bachelors season – Wikipedia (Wikipedia)
Highlight reel from RPI Archives: RPI Archives – 1954 Championship Highlight(digitalassets.archives.rpi.edu)
1961 – Congress recognizes Troy’s “Uncle Sam”
The U.S. House issues House Report 87-1058, saluting Samuel “Uncle Sam” Wilson of Troy as the progenitor of the national symbol “Uncle Sam,” cementing Troy’s claim as the “Home of Uncle Sam.”
Sources:
Congressional document: H. Rept. 87-1058 – “Saluting ‘Uncle Sam’ Wilson, of Troy, N.Y.” (GovInfo)
Local history exhibit: Hart Cluett Museum – Uncle Sam (Hart Cluett Museum)
1967 – Troy Flag Day Parade begins
In response to flag-burning protests, postal worker Jim Pasinella organizes the first Troy Flag Day Paradein 1967; over time it becomes one of the country’s best-known Flag Day parades.
Sources:
Parade history quoted in coverage of its end: Times Union – “Troy’s yearly Flag Day Parade over after 50-year run” (Times Union)
Syndicated version: Chron.com reprint of the same article (Chron)
1980 – Uncle Sam statue dedicated in downtown Troy
A 12-foot aluminum Uncle Sam statue by RPI sculptor George Kratina is dedicated along River Street in Troy in October 1980.
Source (date and description): “5 Spots to Explore the Legacy of Uncle Sam in Troy, New York” – Uncovering New York
1980s – Closure of Cluett, Peabody’s last major Troy plant
Cluett, Peabody & Co., the Arrow shirt manufacturer long headquartered in Troy, closes its last main plant in the city in the 1980s, marking the end of the traditional collar-and-shirt era.
Sources:
History of Troy, NY – LocalWiki (LocalWiki)
Finding aid noting Cluett’s history and closure: Hart Cluett Museum – Finding Aids (Hart Cluett Museum)
1985 – RPI wins second NCAA hockey championship
The 1984–85 RPI Engineers capture their second NCAA Division I men’s ice hockey title with a 2–1 victory over Providence, 31 years after the 1954 championship.
Sources:
1984–85 RPI Engineers season – Wikipedia (Wikipedia)
Anniversary coverage: Times Union – “Looking back at RPI hockey’s 1985 NCAA championship”(Times Union)

2000–Present: Planning, infrastructure & current leadership
2015–2018 – “Realize Troy” comprehensive plan developed
The city undertakes Realize Troy, its first full comprehensive plan in over 50 years, culminating in a plan adopted around 2018 to guide land use, waterfront development, and economic strategy.
Sources:
City planning page linking to the comprehensive plan: City of Troy – Planning & Economic Development (Troy, NY)
Project description: Urban Strategies – Realize Troy (Urban Strategies)
PDF listing: City of Troy – Library of Studies (Realize Troy Comprehensive Plan, May 2018) (Troy, NY)
2021 – Major upgrade of Tomhannock water mains
Troy launches a roughly $40 million project to replace the aging mains from the Tomhannock Reservoir to the city’s water treatment plant, increasing water capacity.
Source (history + project description): Tomhannock Reservoir – Wikipedia (Wikipedia)
November 7, 2023 – Carmella Mantello elected first female mayor of Troy
Carmella Mantello, previously City Council president, is elected Troy’s first woman mayor.
Sources:
Official city page: Mayor’s Office – City of Troy (Troy, NY)
Election coverage: Times Union – “Mantello wins Troy mayoral seat” (Times Union)
January 1–2, 2024 – Mantello sworn in
Mantello takes the oath of office on January 1, 2024, formally becoming the first female mayor in Troy’s history.
Sources:
WAMC – “Troy Mayor Carmella Mantello makes history” (WAMC)
Times Union – “Carmella Mantello takes oath as Troy mayor, first woman to lead city” (Times Union)
2023–2025 – Realignment of City Hall & ongoing initiatives
Mantello’s administration pursues major moves such as relocating Troy City Hall into the historic Proctor’s Theater building downtown and budget initiatives like garbage-fee rollbacks.
Sources:
Times Union – “Mayor: Troy City Hall to move into former Proctor’s Theater” (Times Union)
Times Union – “Troy mayor seeks lower garbage fee in 2026…”
